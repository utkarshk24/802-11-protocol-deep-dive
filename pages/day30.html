Resource Units &
Subcarrier Allocation

A Resource Unit is a block of OFDM subcarriers — the atomic slice OFDMA hands to a client. 802.11ax defines RU sizes from 26 tones up to 996 tones, and a fixed set of ways they tile a channel. Today we map every RU size and how the AP allocates them.

RU Sizes & Client Capacity

802.11ax uses a finer subcarrier spacing (78.125 kHz, ¼ of legacy) so a 20 MHz channel holds 256 tones instead of 64. RUs are defined by tone count:

text RU Sizes and Max Clients per Channel 
RU size   ~bandwidth   Max RUs (clients) per channel width
                        20MHz  40MHz  80MHz  160MHz
26-tone   ~2 MHz          9     18     37     74
52-tone   ~4 MHz          4      8     16     32
106-tone  ~8 MHz          2      4      8     16
242-tone  full 20 MHz     1      2      4      8
484-tone  full 40 MHz     -      1      2      4
996-tone  full 80 MHz     -      -      1      2
2x996     full 160 MHz    -      -      -      1

So a single 80 MHz channel can serve up to 37 clients simultaneously with 26-tone RUs — perfect for many small IoT flows — or one client with a 996-tone RU for max speed.

📐

Tone Plans & the RU Tree

RUs can't be placed arbitrarily — the standard defines a fixed tone plan per channel width. Think of it as a binary tree: a 20 MHz channel is one 242-RU, which can split into two 106-RUs, each into 52-RUs, each into 26-RUs. The AP picks a valid partition and publishes it in HE-SIG-B.

text 20 MHz Tone Plan (one valid split) 
                  [ 242-tone RU ]
                  /              \
          [106-tone]              [106-tone]
          /    |   \              /    |    \
       [52] [26] [52]         [52]  [26]  [52]
        (a null/center 26-tone often reserved near DC)

AP can serve, e.g.:  one 106 (fast client) + four 26 (IoT) in 20 MHz.
RU size is a speed/sharing dial.
Small RUs = many clients, low per-client rate. Large RUs = few clients, high per-client rate. The scheduler balances these per transmission based on who has traffic and how much.
🎛️

Who Allocates RUs?

The AP is the scheduler. For downlink OFDMA it decides the RU partition and which client owns each RU. For uplink OFDMA it sends a Trigger frame (Lesson 33) that assigns each client an RU, MCS, and transmit power, so their uplink transmissions arrive aligned and non-overlapping. This centralized scheduling is the discipline that replaces pure random contention.
