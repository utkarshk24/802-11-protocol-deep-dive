802.11be Overview
& Multi-Link Operation

802.11be (Wi-Fi 7) targets Extremely High Throughput: 320 MHz channels, 4096-QAM, and up to 16 spatial streams. But its signature feature is Multi-Link Operation — a device using 2.4, 5, and 6 GHz simultaneously. Today we open the final phase.

The EHT Headline Features

  • 320 MHz channels: double 802.11ax's 160 MHz, practical only in the wide 6 GHz band.
  • 4096-QAM (4K-QAM): 12 bits/symbol (up from 1024-QAM's 10) — ~20% more raw rate at very high SNR.
  • 16 spatial streams: double 802.11ax, mostly for high-end APs.
  • Multi-Link Operation (MLO): one logical connection spread across multiple bands/links at once.
  • Multi-RU / Preamble Puncturing: a client can be assigned multiple RUs, and the AP can "puncture" a busy sub-channel out of a wide channel instead of abandoning the whole thing.
Preamble puncturing rescues wide channels.
In 802.11ax, one busy 20 MHz sub-channel could force an 80 MHz transmission down to 40 or 20. 802.11be can "punch a hole" around the interference and still use the remaining sub-channels — keeping most of the bandwidth alive.
🔗

Multi-Link Operation (MLO)

MLO is the defining Wi-Fi 7 feature. A Multi-Link Device (MLD) has one MAC address at the logical level but multiple radios ("affiliated" links), and it can use them together:

  • Simultaneous Transmit/Receive (STR): send on 5 GHz while receiving on 6 GHz — true aggregate throughput.
  • Link aggregation: a single traffic flow striped across multiple bands for higher total rate.
  • Reliability / failover: if one band gets congested or hit by interference, traffic shifts to another link seamlessly.
  • Latency: a low-latency flow can pick whichever link is least loaded right now — huge for gaming, AR/VR.
text MLO Concept 
        +-------------------- MLD (one logical device) --------------------+
        |  Link A: 5 GHz  160 MHz   <-- bulk download                       |
        |  Link B: 6 GHz  320 MHz   <-- simultaneous, low-latency traffic   |
        |  Link C: 2.4 GHz          <-- fallback / range                    |
        +-------------------------------------------------------------------+
             One association, one security context, three radios working together.
🔒

MLO and Security

Crucially, MLO establishes a single security association across all links — one SAE/4-way handshake protects the whole MLD, with per-link key derivation. This ties directly into the rest of Phase 5: the WPA3 machinery you're about to study is what secures a Wi-Fi 7 multi-link connection just as it does a single-band one.

🧠 Knowledge Check
What are 802.11be's headline features?

320 MHz channels, 4096-QAM, 16 streams, MLO, and multi-RU/preamble puncturing.

What is Multi-Link Operation (MLO)?

One logical device using multiple bands/links at once (simultaneous TX/RX, aggregation, failover).

How is security handled across MLO links?

A single security association (one SAE/4-way) with per-link key derivation.
