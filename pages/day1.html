Radio Fundamentals: Frequency, Power & Modulation

Frequency: The Foundation

802.11 operates in two unlicensed bands: 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz. (6 GHz is new, we'll cover it later.)

2.4 GHz (2400–2500 MHz): 14 channels, 22 MHz wide each (with overlap). Shared with Bluetooth, baby monitors, microwaves, cordless phones. Congested, but longer range.

5 GHz (5150–5925 MHz): 25+ channels, 20–160 MHz wide (configurable). Less interference, shorter range. Higher frequencies = shorter wavelengths = faster data rates.

Why Frequency Matters

Frequency determines three things: wavelength, propagation distance, and data rate.

  • Wavelength: 2.4 GHz = ~12 cm wavelength. 5 GHz = ~6 cm. Shorter wavelengths are absorbed more easily by obstacles (walls, furniture) → 5 GHz has shorter range.
  • Propagation: 2.4 GHz penetrates walls better. 5 GHz penetrates walls worse but has more spectrum and less interference.
  • Data Rate: 5 GHz can support higher modulation rates (up to 1200 Mbps in 802.11ax) because it has more bandwidth available.

Power: Measured in dBm

Radio power is measured in dBm (decibels relative to 1 milliwatt). It's logarithmic. 

+30 dBm = 1 Watt
+20 dBm = 100 mW
+10 dBm = 10 mW
  0 dBm = 1 mW
-10 dBm = 0.1 mW
-20 dBm = 0.01 mW (very weak)

EIRP (Effective Isotropic Radiated Power) is the actual power transmitted, accounting for antenna gain: EIRP = TxPower + AntennaGain − CableLoss.

Power limits are band- and region-specific — "20 dBm everywhere" is a myth.
In 2.4 GHz, most regions cap EIRP around 20 dBm (100 mW). But 5 GHz is far more generous: U-NII-1/U-NII-3 allow up to 30 dBm (1 W) EIRP in the US, and DFS bands (U-NII-2) sit in between, often with transmit-power-control (TPC) requirements. Always check the regulatory domain (country code) — it changes both the legal channels and the power ceiling. We cover DFS/TPC (802.11h) later.

Signal Strength: RSSI & Path Loss

RSSI (Received Signal Strength Indicator) is measured in dBm. It's negative. A device 1 meter away: -40 dBm. A device 50 meters away: -85 dBm.

Path loss is why. Radio signals weaken as distance increases. In free space (no obstacles), signal strength drops 20 dB for every 10× increase in distance. In a building with walls, it's worse. 

Free Space Path Loss (dB) = 20 * log10(distance) + 20 * log10(frequency) - 147.55

Example: 1 meter at 2.4 GHz = ~40 dB loss
         10 meters at 2.4 GHz = ~60 dB loss
         100 meters at 2.4 GHz = ~80 dB loss

Modulation: BPSK, QPSK, 16-QAM, 64-QAM

Modulation converts digital data into radio waves. Different modulations have different speeds and range tradeoffs.

ModulationData Rate (OFDM)RobustnessRange
BPSK (1 bit/symbol)6 MbpsVery robustLongest
QPSK (2 bits/symbol)12–24 MbpsRobustLong
16-QAM (4 bits/symbol)48–54 MbpsMediumMedium
64-QAM (6 bits/symbol)108–150 MbpsFragileShort

Note: the rates above assume a single spatial stream on a 20 MHz channel with an 800 ns guard interval. Widen the channel (40/80/160 MHz), add spatial streams, or shorten the guard interval and the same modulation yields far higher rates — that's the whole story of 802.11n/ac/ax, which we build up over the course.

Why the tradeoff? More bits per symbol = smaller spacing between signal states in the constellation. In a noisy environment (low SNR), those states get confused and bit errors spike. BPSK is robust; 64-QAM (and later 256/1024-QAM) needs a clean, high-SNR signal. This is why devices rate-adapt: they drop to a lower-order modulation as you walk away from the AP.

SNR: The Number That Actually Decides Your Rate

RSSI alone doesn't determine speed — SNR (Signal-to-Noise Ratio) does. SNR (dB) = RSSI − NoiseFloor. A typical noise floor is around −95 dBm, so an RSSI of −70 dBm gives ~25 dB SNR. Each modulation needs a minimum SNR to decode reliably:

ModulationApprox. min SNRTypical usable RSSI
BPSK~5 dB−90 dBm
QPSK~10 dB−85 dBm
16-QAM~18 dB−78 dBm
64-QAM~25 dB−70 dBm
256-QAM (11ac)~30 dB−65 dBm
1024-QAM (11ax)~35 dB−60 dBm

Channels: Overlapping vs. Non-Overlapping

2.4 GHz: 14 channels, 5 MHz apart, each 22 MHz wide. They overlap heavily. Only channels 1, 6, 11 are non-overlapping in the USA.

5 GHz: Channels are 5 MHz apart. Multiple non-overlapping channels: 36–48, 52–144, 149–165, etc.

Why non-overlapping matters: If two APs use overlapping channels, they interfere. Devices hear both and get confused. Rate drops, latency increases, connections fail.

Real-World Example: Reading an RSSI Chart

You're troubleshooting why a device won't connect to an AP 50 meters away. You measure RSSI: -82 dBm. AP transmit power: 20 dBm. Antenna gain: 6 dBi. Expected path loss at 50m: ~75 dB. Actual received power: 20 + 6 - 75 = -49 dBm. But you're seeing -82 dBm.

Protocol understanding: The extra 33 dB loss means there are obstacles. Maybe the path crosses a reinforced concrete wall (-15 dB) and a metal door (-18 dB). Or there's heavy interference on this channel. The device is trying to connect using 64-QAM (requires -65 dBm), but it's only seeing -82 dBm. It should fall back to QPSK (-80 dBm range). If it's not, that's a modulation fallback bug in the driver.

Without protocol knowledge, you just say "move closer." With it, you diagnose the root cause.

Compute It: A Link-Budget Calculator

Let's turn the theory into a tool. This calculator estimates free-space path loss, the received signal, the resulting SNR, and the best modulation the link can sustain — the exact reasoning a site-survey engineer does in their head.

python link_budget.py 
import math

def fspl_db(distance_m, freq_mhz):
    """Free-space path loss in dB (distance in m, freq in MHz)."""
    return 20*math.log10(distance_m) + 20*math.log10(freq_mhz) + 32.44

def link_budget(tx_dbm, tx_gain, rx_gain, distance_m, freq_mhz,
                noise_floor=-95):
    loss = fspl_db(distance_m, freq_mhz)
    rssi = tx_dbm + tx_gain + rx_gain - loss
    snr  = rssi - noise_floor
    # pick the densest modulation the SNR supports
    tiers = [(35, "1024-QAM"), (30, "256-QAM"), (25, "64-QAM"),
             (18, "16-QAM"),   (10, "QPSK"),    (5, "BPSK")]
    mod = next((m for s, m in tiers if snr >= s), "no link")
    return rssi, snr, mod

for d in (1, 10, 50, 100):
    rssi, snr, mod = link_budget(tx_dbm=20, tx_gain=3, rx_gain=2,
                                 distance_m=d, freq_mhz=2437)  # ch 6
    print(f"{d:4d} m  RSSI={rssi:6.1f} dBm  SNR={snr:5.1f} dB  -> {mod}")

Note: 32.44 is the FSPL constant when distance is in km and frequency in MHz; using metres shifts the constant, so this simplified model is for relative comparison, not regulatory-grade prediction. Real surveys add wall/obstacle losses on top.

Exercise: Build Your Intuition

  • Task 1: Run link_budget.py and find the distance at which your link drops from 256-QAM to 64-QAM. Then re-run at 5 GHz (freq_mhz=5180) — how much does the usable range shrink for the same power?
  • Task 2: Your phone shows RSSI −72 dBm with a noise floor of −92 dBm. What is the SNR, and which modulation (from the table above) is realistic? Would 256-QAM work?
  • Task 3: An AP transmits at 23 dBm with a 5 dBi antenna and 2 dB of cable loss. What is its EIRP? Is that legal on a 2.4 GHz channel in the US (≤ 36 dBm EIRP cap, but note the conducted-power rules)?
Hint: SNR = RSSI − noise floor. For Task 2 that's −72 − (−92) = 20 dB, which comfortably supports 16-QAM and marginally 64-QAM, but not the ~30 dB that 256-QAM wants — so a device here would rate-adapt down.
