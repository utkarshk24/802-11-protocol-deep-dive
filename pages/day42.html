Protection (MFP)

Encrypting data frames while leaving management frames unauthenticated is exactly why deauth floods work (Lesson 14). 802.11w Management Frame Protection closes that gap — and WPA3 makes it mandatory. Today: protected robust management frames, BIP, and the SA Query.

What MFP Protects

802.11w protects robust management frames — the ones that can disrupt a connection if forged:

  • Deauthentication & Disassociation: the classic spoofing targets.
  • Robust Action frames: QoS, Block Ack, radio measurement, FT, etc.

Two kinds of protection depending on the frame's addressing:

  • Unicast robust mgmt frames: encrypted + integrity-protected with the pairwise TK (same CCMP/GCMP as data).
  • Broadcast/multicast robust mgmt frames: integrity-protected (not encrypted) using BIP with the IGTK.
Beacons and probe responses are NOT protected.
MFP covers deauth/disassoc/robust-action, not discovery frames. Beacons stay open so new clients can find the network — which is why beacon-based evil-twin and rogue-AP detection (Lesson 17, Lesson 43) still matter even with MFP enabled.
🔏

BIP & the IGTK

BIP (Broadcast/Multicast Integrity Protocol) adds a Management MIC element (MMIE) to group-addressed management frames, computed with the IGTK (Integrity Group Temporal Key), distributed alongside the GTK in the 4-way handshake. Variants:

  • BIP-CMAC-128: AES-CMAC, the WPA2/WPA3 baseline.
  • BIP-GMAC-256: AES-GMAC, used in WPA3-Enterprise 192-bit (Lesson 39).

A replay counter in the MMIE also stops replayed broadcast management frames.

The SA Query Mechanism

MFP creates a subtle problem: if a real client crashes and tries to reconnect, the AP (which still thinks it's associated) might see the new association request as a spoof. The SA Query resolves this:

text SA Query Flow 
Unexpected (Re)Assoc Req arrives while STA is "associated"
   AP -> STA   SA Query Request  (protected action frame)
      |
      +-- real STA still there?  -> replies SA Query Response
      |     -> AP keeps existing association, rejects the new req
      |
      +-- no response (was a spoof / stale)?
            -> AP tears down old SA, allows fresh association

Because SA Query frames are themselves protected, an attacker can't forge the response — so a spoofed reassociation can't hijack or drop a legitimate session.

⚙️

MFP Modes (RSN Capabilities)

The RSN IE carries two bits: MFPC (Management Frame Protection Capable) and MFPR (Required). Combinations give three deployment modes: disabled, optional (protect clients that support it, allow those that don't), and required (WPA3's stance — clients must support MFP or they can't join).
