Day 28 Phase 4 · 802.11ax (WiFi 6)

Why 802.11ax
Was Revolutionary

802.11ax (Wi-Fi 6, 2019) changed the goal. Where every prior amendment chased peak speed, 802.11ax chased efficiency in crowded networks — many devices sharing airtime well. OFDMA, uplink multi-user, TWT, and BSS coloring are all about the many, not the one.

A Different Objective

802.11ac made one client fast. But a stadium, a lecture hall, or a dense apartment block has hundreds of clients, most sending tiny packets. There, the bottleneck isn't peak rate — it's contention overhead: everyone fighting for the channel one frame at a time. 802.11ax (High Efficiency, HE) attacks that directly.

"High Efficiency," not "Very High Throughput."
The amendment name tells the story. HE improves average throughput per user in dense deployments by 4× not through faster peak rates, but by using the channel far more efficiently when many devices share it.
🧩

The Five Big Innovations

  • OFDMA: Subdivide a channel into Resource Units so multiple clients transmit in the same PPDU on different subcarriers — like sharing a highway by lanes instead of taking turns. (Days 29–31)
  • Uplink MU-MIMO & MU-OFDMA: The AP schedules multiple clients to transmit upstream simultaneously — new in 802.11ax. (Day 33)
  • 1024-QAM: 10 bits/symbol (up from 256-QAM's 8), a ~25% raw rate bump at high SNR.
  • Target Wake Time (TWT): The AP and client negotiate scheduled wake windows, hugely improving battery life for IoT. (Day 32)
  • BSS Coloring: Tag frames with a "color" so a device can ignore overlapping-BSS traffic and transmit sooner, raising spatial reuse. (Day 34)
📡

Both Bands, Plus 6 GHz

Unlike 802.11ac (5 GHz only), 802.11ax runs on both 2.4 and 5 GHz, bringing OFDMA and 1024-QAM to the older band. Wi-Fi 6E extends 802.11ax into the new 6 GHz band (up to 1200 MHz of clean spectrum in many regions), where wide 160 MHz channels are finally practical without DFS congestion.
