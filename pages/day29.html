OFDM vs OFDMA

In OFDM (802.11a/n/ac), a transmission uses all the channel's subcarriers for one device at a time. In OFDMA, the subcarriers are grouped into Resource Units (RUs), and different RUs can carry data for different devices simultaneously.

text The Highway Analogy 
OFDM  (802.11ac):  one truck uses the whole road, others wait
  [========= CLIENT A =========]   then
  [========= CLIENT B =========]

OFDMA (802.11ax):  the road has lanes, several go at once
  [== A ==][==== B ====][= C =][== D ==]   all in one PPDU
OFDMA crushes small-packet overhead.
A 200-byte IoT status update no longer needs to seize the entire channel. Dozens of tiny transmissions ride together in one PPDU, so per-frame contention and inter-frame spacing are paid once instead of dozens of times. That's the efficiency win.
🧱

The Four HE PPDU Formats

  • HE SU: single-user, one device gets the channel (like VHT SU).
  • HE MU: multi-user downlink — OFDMA and/or MU-MIMO to several clients at once.
  • HE Trigger-Based (TB): uplink transmissions from multiple clients, coordinated by a Trigger frame from the AP.
  • HE ER SU: Extended Range single-user, for far-away clients (extra preamble robustness).
📻

HE Preamble & SIG Fields

Like VHT, HE keeps a legacy preamble for coexistence, then adds HE-specific fields:

text HE MU PPDU Preamble 
L-STF, L-LTF, L-SIG   legacy (coexistence + NAV)
RL-SIG                 Repeated L-SIG -> "this is an HE frame"
HE-SIG-A              common params: BW, GI+LTF, TXOP, BSS color...
HE-SIG-B             (MU only) RU allocation + per-user MCS/streams
HE-STF, HE-LTF        training for HE / MIMO
Data                  the RU-partitioned payload

HE-SIG-B is the OFDMA brain: its Common field publishes the RU allocation pattern, and its per-user fields tell each client which RU is theirs and at what MCS. Without HE-SIG-B, a client wouldn't know which slice of the channel to decode.
