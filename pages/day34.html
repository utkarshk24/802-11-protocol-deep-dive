What Is a BSS Color?

A BSS Color is a 6-bit identifier (1–63) the AP assigns to its BSS and stamps into the HE-SIG-A of every HE frame. A receiving device can read the color early in the preamble and instantly know: "this frame belongs to my network (intra-BSS)" or "this is a neighbor's network (inter-BSS / OBSS)."

Color = a fast "is this mine?" tag.
Before BSS coloring, a device had to treat every detectable frame as a reason to defer. With coloring, it distinguishes its own BSS from overlapping ones in the first microseconds of the preamble and can make a smarter decision about whether to wait.
♻️

Spatial Reuse & Adaptive CCA

Once frames are colored, 802.11ax enables spatial reuse via an adjustable Clear Channel Assessment threshold (OBSS_PD, Overlapping BSS Packet Detect):

  • Normally a device defers if it detects any signal above the CCA threshold (~ -82 dBm).
  • With spatial reuse, if a frame is identified as inter-BSS (different color) and is weak (below a higher OBSS_PD threshold, e.g. -72 dBm), the device may ignore it and transmit anyway.
  • To avoid interfering with that distant BSS, it also caps its transmit power — a power/threshold trade managed together.
text Spatial Reuse Decision 
Detect frame -> read BSS color
  |
  +-- same color (intra-BSS)?   -> normal CSMA/CA, defer as usual
  |
  +-- different color (OBSS)?
        |
        +-- signal < OBSS_PD threshold (weak, far away)?
        |     -> IGNORE it, transmit now (cap TX power)
        |
        +-- signal strong (close neighbor)?
              -> defer normally (don't cause a collision)

The HE Operation Element & Color Collisions

The HE Operation element in beacons carries the BSS Color info, including the current color and a "BSS Color Disabled" bit. Because there are only 63 colors, two nearby APs can pick the same one — a color collision. When an AP detects this (via reports from its clients), it can announce a color change, much like a channel switch. This keeps the "is this mine?" test reliable even as networks come and go.
