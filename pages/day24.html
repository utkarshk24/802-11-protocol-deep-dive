80 MHz &
160 MHz Channels

Channel bonding is 802.11ac's most direct speed lever: gluing 20 MHz channels into 80 or 160 MHz doubles then quadruples throughput. But wide channels mean fewer non-overlapping options, more interference, and DFS headaches. Today we cover the mechanics and the trade-offs.

How Bonding Works

802.11ac builds wide channels from 20 MHz building blocks: two 20s make a 40, two 40s make an 80, two 80s make a 160 (or two non-contiguous 80s make an "80+80"). One 20 MHz block is the primary channel; management and beacons live there, and legacy clients camp on it.

[20][20][20][20][20][20][20][20]   eight 20 MHz channels
[  40  ][  40  ][  40  ][  40  ]   40 MHz pairs
[      80      ][      80      ]   80 MHz
[            160            ]      160 MHz

Primary 20 -> Primary 40 -> Primary 80  (nested)
Secondary channels are added around the primary.

Primary, Secondary & CCA

Before transmitting on a wide channel, the AP must find the whole thing idle. It runs Clear Channel Assessment (CCA) on the primary 20 (full sensitivity) and on the secondary segments (energy detect). If a secondary is busy, the AP falls back to a narrower width rather than waiting — dynamic bandwidth operation.

Wide channels are fragile in crowded air.
An 80 MHz transmission needs 80 MHz of silence. In an apartment building with a dozen APs, a single neighbor on your secondary channel forces you down to 40 or 20 MHz. This is why "just set 160 MHz" often lowers real throughput in dense environments.
📡

DFS and the 160 MHz Problem

In the US/EU, only a handful of 20 MHz channels sit outside the DFS (radar) bands. To form a contiguous 160 MHz channel you almost always must include DFS channels, which means:

  • A mandatory Channel Availability Check (typically 60 s, up to 10 min on weather-radar channels) before first use.
  • Continuous radar monitoring — a detection forces an immediate Channel Switch Announcement (Lesson 15).
  • Many client devices historically avoided or poorly supported DFS/160 MHz, so the AP may never actually get to use it.

The practical result: 80 MHz is the sweet spot for most 802.11ac deployments. 160 MHz shines in clean spectrum (labs, point-to-point, some enterprise) but is rarely dependable at home.

Exercise: Channel Planning

  • Task 1: Scan your 5 GHz band. How many APs are on 80 MHz? How many overlap your primary?
  • Task 2: Identify which of your usable channels are DFS. Could you build a non-DFS 80 MHz channel?
  • Task 3: Force your AP to 160 MHz, then to 40 MHz, and benchmark real throughput at close and far range. Which wins where?
🧠 Knowledge Check
How is an 80 MHz channel built?

By bonding four 20 MHz blocks; one of them is the primary channel.

Why do wide channels struggle in dense areas?

The whole width must be idle; a busy secondary forces a fallback to a narrower width.

Why is 160 MHz hard in practice?

It usually includes DFS channels - CAC delay, radar eviction, and weak client support.
